INCIDENT RESPONSE PLAYBOOK: BRUTE FORCE LOGIN ATTEMPT

THREAT TYPE: Brute Force / Credential Stuffing
SEVERITY: Medium to High

DESCRIPTION:
A brute force attack involves an attacker (or automated script) repeatedly
attempting to log in to an account or service using many different
password combinations. This is detected via an abnormally high number of
failed login attempts within a short time window, often targeting a single
account or a shared service such as RDP, SSH, or a web login form.

WHY IT IS DANGEROUS:
1. Account Compromise: Given enough attempts, weak or reused passwords
   can eventually be guessed, granting the attacker access.
2. Lateral Movement Risk: A compromised account can be used to pivot
   deeper into the network.
3. Service Disruption: High-volume login attempts can degrade
   authentication service performance for legitimate users.
4. Precursor to Larger Attack: Brute forcing is often a reconnaissance
   or initial-access step before ransomware or data exfiltration.

IMMEDIATE MITIGATION STEPS:
1. Temporarily lock or rate-limit the targeted account to stop further
   guessing attempts.
2. Block or throttle the source IP address(es) at the firewall level.
3. Enforce multi-factor authentication (MFA) on the affected account
   and, ideally, organization-wide.
4. Review authentication logs to confirm whether any attempt succeeded;
   if so, treat the account as compromised and force a password reset.
5. Enable account lockout policies after N failed attempts.
6. Consider deploying fail2ban-style tooling or a Web Application
   Firewall (WAF) rule to automatically block repeat offenders.
7. Notify the user of the affected account and advise a password change.

DETECTION INDICATORS:
- Multiple (5+) failed login attempts within a short time window.
- Failed attempts originating from the same source in rapid succession.
- Login attempts outside normal working hours or from unusual locations.
